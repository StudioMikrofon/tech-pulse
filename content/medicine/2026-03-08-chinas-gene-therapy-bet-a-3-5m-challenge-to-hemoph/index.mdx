In April 2023, China’s National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) approved the first domestically developed gene therapy for [hemophilia B](https://www.hemophilia.org/bleeding-disorders-a-z/types/hemophilia-b), a rare inherited bleeding disorder. The treatment, developed by [Belief BioMed](https://www.beliefbiomed.com/), marks a deliberate step into one of the most expensive corners of modern medicine—where single-dose therapies can cost millions per patient. The approval isn’t just a regulatory milestone; it’s a calculated move in a global pharmaceutical chess match where pricing, access, and national biotech ambition collide.

The context is impossible to ignore. Hemophilia B, caused by a deficiency in clotting factor IX, has long been managed with lifelong infusions of replacement proteins—costly, burdensome, and imperfect. Gene therapy promises a one-time fix by delivering a functional copy of the faulty gene via a modified virus. The problem? The only other approved gene therapy for hemophilia B, [Hemgenix](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-approves-first-gene-therapy-treat-adults-hemophilia-b) (etranacogene dezaparvovec), carries a U.S. list price of [$3.5 million](https://www.statnews.com/2022/12/01/csl-behring-hemgenix-gene-therapy-hemophilia-b-price/)—the most expensive drug in the world. China’s approval of Belief BioMed’s therapy, while not yet priced, signals an intent to disrupt this market dynamic.

What we know with certainty is limited but critical. The NMPA’s green light is based on **regulatory-grade evidence** (Phase I/II and III trials, per [company statements](https://www.beliefbiomed.com/pipeline)), though full trial data remain unpublished in peer-reviewed journals. Early reports suggest the therapy uses an adeno-associated virus (AAV) vector—standard for gene therapies—but with proprietary modifications to improve efficiency. The **sample size** in pivotal trials appears modest (likely under 100 patients, typical for rare-disease studies), raising questions about long-term durability and safety in broader populations. Unlike Hemgenix, which targets adults, Belief BioMed’s therapy may include pediatric indications, though this isn’t confirmed.

The approval arrives amid China’s broader push to [reduce dependence on foreign pharmaceuticals](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-00520-1), particularly in high-cost areas like oncology and genetic diseases. Gene therapies, with their sky-high price tags and complex manufacturing, are a prime target. Yet the strategy carries risks: AAV-based treatments can trigger immune responses, and long-term data (beyond 5–10 years) are scarce even for approved products like [Luxturna](https://www.fda.gov/vaccines-blood-biologics/cellular-gene-therapy-products/luxturna) or [Zolgensma](https://www.fda.gov/vaccines-blood-biologics/cellular-gene-therapy-products/zolgensma). For patients, the stakes are existential—gene therapy’s promise of a ‘cure’ must be weighed against unknowns that no trial, however rigorous, can fully address in its first decade.

For now, the most pressing question isn’t whether Belief BioMed’s therapy works—**regulatory approval confirms it meets China’s standards for safety and efficacy**—but what it means for the 100,000+ people worldwide living with hemophilia B. The therapy’s **clinical relevance** hinges on three unresolved factors: cost, durability, and access. If priced significantly below Hemgenix (as early industry chatter suggests), it could pressure Western manufacturers to adjust pricing or accelerate similar cost-cutting innovations. But ‘significantly below’ $3.5 million still leaves room for a seven-figure price tag—far beyond the reach of most patients without robust insurance or government subsidies.

Then there’s the issue of **regulatory divergence**. China’s NMPA and the U.S. FDA or EMA don’t always align on evidence thresholds. Hemgenix’s approval, for instance, relied on a single-arm trial with 54 patients; Belief BioMed’s data may follow a similar path. This isn’t inherently problematic—rare diseases often necessitate flexible trial designs—but it underscores that ‘approved’ doesn’t mean ‘equally proven’ across jurisdictions. Patients in China may soon have a new option, but those in the U.S. or EU will need to wait for separate submissions and reviews, a process that could take years.

The approval also tests China’s ability to **scale advanced biomanufacturing**. Gene therapies require highly specialized production, and even approved products like [Zolgensma](https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/novartis-zolgensma-gene-therapy-shortage-us-due-manufacturing-woes-2023-06-14/) have faced shortages due to manufacturing bottlenecks. Belief BioMed has partnered with domestic CDMOs (contract development and manufacturing organizations), but whether these can meet demand—especially if the therapy expands to other Asian markets—remains unproven.

What we don’t know still dwarf what we do. Will the therapy’s effects last a decade or more, as hoped? Will immune responses limit its use in certain patients? And critically, will China’s pricing model—likely tied to its national healthcare system—translate to affordability elsewhere? The answers will determine whether this approval is a **localized cost-control measure** or the first shot in a global shift toward more accessible gene therapies.

One thing is clear: the era of million-dollar-one-time cures is here to stay. The question is no longer *if* these therapies will exist, but *who* will control their development, pricing, and distribution. China’s move suggests it intends to be a player—not just a market—in that future.
